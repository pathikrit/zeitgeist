Macro/Geopolitical Overview

Trump's Aggressive Policy Shifts

  • The administration's pursuit of long-term federal control over DC policing and the ability to cut billions from foreign aid point to lasting uncertainty regarding the US government’s role domestically and internationally.
    • Second-order risk: Further centralization could politicize security apparatus, affect domestic unrest, and impact sectors dependent on federal and local spending.
    • Foreign aid reductions: If sustained, expect negative knock-on effects for certain emerging market economies (trade partners, NGOs), possibly yielding volatility in currency markets and international indices.

US-Russia-Ukraine Diplomacy

  • The Trump-Putin summit, especially with reports of minerals-for-peace negotiations and direct financial incentives, injects unpredictability into commodity markets, defense spending, and the global security outlook.
    • Third-order consequences: If peace deals hinge on US mineral interests (e.g. rare earths, metals), this could drive significant sector reallocation within commodities, mining, and related equities.
  • Ukraine’s continuing diplomatic efforts signal that G7/EU defense and humanitarian commitments will remain front and center.
    • Potential outcomes: Any appeasement could depress defense stocks (if war risks abate), or drive further sanctions/defense escalation if talks fall through.

Middle East Escalations

  • Israeli actions and new West Bank settlements are further straining the prospect of regional peace, with near-term risk of expanded conflict.
    • Sector impact: Energy prices remain sensitive to regional instability. Defense companies could benefit amid rising global security spend.

US Domestic Politics and Policy

  • Redistricting battles and legal maneuvering (especially in large states like California and Texas) continue to inject policy unpredictability and could impact not just municipalities but also national legislative priorities (e.g., healthcare, climate policy, tech regulation).

Economic & Policy Developments

Inflation & Fed Policy

Public Health & Consumption

  • Vaccine skepticism and NIH retreat from mRNA contracts point to potential long-term changes in public health policy and pharma R&D focus.
    • Third-order risk: Erosion of trust in pharmaceuticals could slow adoption of innovative treatments, rewiring sector outlooks (biotech, healthcare insurers).
