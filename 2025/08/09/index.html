Macro & Economic Outlook

US Rates, Inflation, and Recession Risks

  • The current expectation is for at least one Federal Reserve rate cut by year-end, with multiple cuts viewed as somewhat less likely but not out of the question. No significant probability is assigned to a hike or "emergency" cut.
  • US recession risk in 2025 appears to be low, with markets comfortable moving ahead with a soft-landing scenario.
  • Inflation near or above 3% remains plausible, with attenuated risk of a surprise spike. However, most do not forecast levels above 4% or 5%; the moderate inflation backdrop supports risk assets but maintains elevated uncertainty around rates and duration.
  • Ongoing tariff debates and threats of further protectionist measures (notably from Trump) add potential upside risk to inflation and downside risk to global supply chain reliability.

Second-order effects

  • Persistent, but not extreme, inflation supports relative outperformance for companies with pricing power—especially in sectors such as software, branded consumer goods, and select healthcare.
  • Mild disinflation and rate-cut expectations keep downward pressure on short-duration yields while supporting longer-duration bonds and equity multiples.
  • If Trump’s protectionist policy stance takes hold, cyclical sectors (autos, industrials, consumer durables) could face higher costs; beneficiaries could include domestic energy, North American manufacturing and agribusiness, and commodities.

Geopolitics & Systemic Risk

  • The imminent Trump-Putin meeting increases headline risk around global risk assets—particularly energy, defense, and commodities.
  • The escalation in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and Germany's partial arms embargo introduce supply chain challenges for global defense and could drive shocks in energy and commodities.
  • Fears of cartel-related violence and US cross-border action add idiosyncratic risk for Latin American assets and supply chains.

Second-order effects

  • Elevated geopolitical risk often supports defense names and, in the event of actual armed escalation, benefits energy and certain commodity exposures.
  • Emerging market exposures (especially Mexico, Israel, parts of Europe) demand heightened scrutiny.

Sector & Thematic Spotlights

Technology & Mega-Cap Performance

  • Nvidia is favored to remain the world’s most valuable company at year-end, with Microsoft trailing and Apple lagging further.
  • Executive turnover probabilities at the largest techs remain low; leadership continuity is expected at Apple, Google, and Amazon.
  • AI sector leadership remains contested. OpenAI, xAI, and Microsoft are seen as principal contenders, but no single firm has a dominant chance to claim top AI performance benchmarks by year-end.
  • Alphabet’s struggles with Google Gemini and underwhelming AI outputs may depress sentiment in AI-adjacent search/advertising valuations.

Second-order effects

  • Continued mega-cap tech leadership, especially for Nvidia, suggests persistent flows into semiconductors, hardware suppliers, and related cloud and AI infrastructure businesses.
  • Increasing attention on digital advertising margins and contest from Amazon may pressure ad tech, illustrated by The Trade Desk's sharp selloff.
  • AI leadership ambiguity suggests VC and public AI bets should be diversified; no "winner takes all" is clearly anticipated.

Crypto & Digital Assets

  • Strong likelihood for additional crypto ETFs (Ripple, Litecoin, Cardano, Dogecoin) to receive approval by year-end, supporting further institutional adoption and liquidity.
  • Bitcoin’s path to aggressive new highs ($150k+) is not assured, but moderate appreciation (above $130k) is viewed as likely for the next 12-18 months, barring a systemic event.
  • Confidence in Tether’s solvency and peg stability remains high, reducing overall systemic risk for exchanges and DeFi, though headline risk persists.

Second-order effects

  • ETF approvals may drive temporary momentum boosts for altcoins.
  • Lack of widespread concern about crypto-backed systemic failures encourages further TradFi-integration and expansion.

Commodities

  • Gold is expected to appreciate meaningfully through 2025; closing above $3,200 seems likely, with very low probability of falling below current levels. Policy uncertainty, rate cuts, and geopolitical risks are tailwinds.
  • Ongoing tariff confusion could add volatility, but the underlying bid looks robust.

Thematic Risk—Climate, Health, Urban Policy

  • US hurricane activity for the 2025 Atlantic season is expected to be elevated, supporting higher than average claims for reinsurers and property insurance REITs.
  • Covid-19 and emerging health crises (e.g., ‘Stratus’ variant, flesh-eating bacteria, vector-borne illnesses in China) are gaining public health attention, but catastrophic economic impact is not anticipated at this stage.
  • Urban policy headlines (NYC mayor, UCLA settlement, IRS turnover) merit attention for municipal and university-linked bond exposures but convey limited near-term market risk.

Company & Individual Names to Watch

  • Nvidia led mega-cap tech continues to dominate index weighting and sentiment.
  • Intel faces political and internal headwinds; leadership risk and US chip policy test continue.
  • Trade Desk and the ad-tech sector: performance at risk due to competitive threats from Amazon and AI-powered shifts in marketing platforms.
  • Paramount/Skydance: merger completed, but operational focus now on talent retention and synergy realization; sector-wide consolidation could accelerate.
  • MicroStrategy is not expected to liquidate Bitcoin holdings, keeping it as a leveraged BTC play.

Key Watch Points (Short-Term)

  • Any sharp changes to Fed rate outlook or inflation data that diverge from the current consensus.
  • Results from the Trump-Putin meeting and any escalation of geopolitical events in Ukraine, Gaza, or US-Mexico relations.
  • Effects and fallout from US tariff and gold policy clarifications; further policy signals from Trump administration.
  • Corporate earnings and guidance from mega-cap tech, ad tech, and defense sector firms.
  • Named storm developments for insurers and related bond sectors.

Summary

The near-term US outlook remains constructive for equities with a preference for large-cap tech and selected inflation/classic defensives, barring a significant geopolitical shock. Persistent geopolitical noise, evolving US policy rhetoric, and rising global health alerts introduce headline risk, but don't yet challenge the prevailing "risk-on" mentality. Commodities and digital assets continue to offer both inflation protection and speculative upside. Keep a close eye on rapid policy pivots, top-level executive moves at major techs, and sectoral winners in AI and defense as risk appetite remains robust.
