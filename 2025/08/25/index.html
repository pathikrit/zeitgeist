Daily Memo (25-Aug-2025)

Top News Summary

  • Israeli airstrikes in Gaza have killed journalists and medical staff, heightening Middle East risk.
  • U.S. National Guard units begin carrying firearms in D.C.; Chicago officials push back on military deployment plans.
  • France formally protests to the U.S. over an ambassador’s letter on antisemitism.
  • U.S. government cancels $2.4B in Harvard research grants, escalating campus politics tensions.
  • U.S. government halts Orsted offshore wind project; Orsted shares plunge on regulatory/capex risks.
  • Keurig Dr Pepper announced $18B JDE Peet’s acquisition, indicating ongoing consolidation in food/beverage.
  • Natural disasters: Vietnam evacuating 500,000 people ahead of Typhoon Kajiki.
  • Disease/health watch: First U.S. case of flesh-eating screwworm confirmed; measles cases in Texas/NJ rising.

Macro & Policy

U.S. Federal Reserve and Rates

  • The Fed is unlikely to hike rates in 2025, with minimal probability of an emergency rate cut or aggressive multi-cut cycles. A gradual path of 1–2 cuts remains plausible; rates may still be somewhat restrictive (>3.5%) by late 2025.
  • Market consensus is for rates to decline slowly through 2026, remaining above 3.25% by late that year. The high-for-longer narrative has diminished, supporting risk assets and longer-duration treasuries.
  • Subtle dovish signals out of the Fed’s Jackson Hole stance, with markets focusing on cooling inflation and avoiding recession-level cuts.

Second-order effects:

  • Stable/declining rates ease financial conditions for corporates—likely supportive to large-cap equities, especially tech/growth.
  • High and rising government debt levels (above $38T by year-end) and more than $500B in new federal outlays expected in 2025 continue to pressure long-end rates, possibly leading to curve steepening.
  • Credit rating risk is slightly elevated but not likely (another downgrade would add volatility, especially in Treasuries and MBS).

Fiscal & Political

  • Large U.S. deficit spending persists; a $1T+ expansion is possible but not baseline.
  • Trump administration expected to bring a hawkish trade stance: high probability of new tariffs on China, Mexico, Canada, Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, and parts of the EU; copper tariffs specifically are favored.
  • Pharma tariffs are now a coin flip, raising healthcare sector input cost risks.
  • Regulatory volatility remains, particularly in tax policy, crypto legislation, and university/government research funding (Harvard case as a flashpoint).

Second-order effects:

  • Tariffs will increase input costs in manufacturing, autos, tech hardware, metals, and pharmaceuticals, potentially introducing margin headwinds and spurring near-term supply chain adjustments.
  • Tariffs could also boost U.S. domestic manufacturing (notably in semiconductors) and make select commodities (copper) more volatile.
  • Large fiscal deficits may keep inflation risk simmering in the background even if headline CPI trends lower.

Geopolitics

  • Renewed Gaza conflict increases Middle East instability, creating a short-term risk premium for energy and defense stocks.
  • U.S.-EU tensions rise over campus speech/funding and Israel/Palestinian policy.
  • Low odds of eurozone expansion or abrupt changes in Eastern European governments.

Sector Focus

Technology

  • Tech sector will likely experience more layoffs in 2025 than in 2024, especially outside the AI/semiconductor "hot spots."
  • U.S. semiconductor industry set for robust >7% production growth in 2025, driven by ongoing CHIPS Act spending and public sector support (U.S. government is expected to take a direct stake in Intel).
  • Leadership at U.S. tech giants (Apple, Google, OpenAI) are stable.
  • Hardware innovation remains robust (Apple, Samsung, Google) but beware consumer demand headwinds outside flagship models.

Second-order effects:

  • AI and chips remain core growth engines; supplier ecosystem and capex beneficiaries (semicap equipment, industrial automation) stand to benefit further.
  • U.S. tariffs and industrial policy may spur further onshoring of technology components and modular supply chains; negative for select Asian and EU hardware firms.

Energy & Materials

  • Expect lower oil prices into year-end (WTI below $65 likely) due to global demand concerns and alternative supply, despite recent geopolitics. Gasoline prices in Texas have a nontrivial chance of topping $3.
  • Natural gas prices are poised for a strong rally ($4+ baseline expected for 2025).
  • Oil, copper, and other commodity-exposed sectors face risks from U.S. protectionism (tariffs) and rising alternative energy investment.
  • Solar energy installations in the U.S. are slowing relative to recent growth, but the 40–50 GWdc yearly range remains achievable.
  • Risks to offshore wind developers have escalated (e.g., Orsted), with U.S. federal intervention halting major projects—watch for ripple effects to other developers and suppliers.

Healthcare

  • Pharma sector could face input cost increases and trade friction if Trump tariffs are enacted; monitor global supply chains for essential APIs and devices.
  • U.S. focus on vaccine-preventable diseases (measles uptick) and pandemic-adjacent news (flesh-eating parasite) signal elevated regulatory/defensive interest in public health companies.

Crypto

  • Major legislative reform on U.S. crypto market structure is possible this year; de minimis tax exemption and capital gains tax elimination seem unlikely.
  • Crypto remains fundamentally volatile but positive-skewed: Bitcoin over $100K this year is near-certain, with potential to test $130K–$150K by year-end. Ethereum could reach the $5–6K+ range.
  • Apple appears unlikely (but not impossible) to add USDC payments this year, so retail stablecoin adoption news not imminent.

Broad Markets

  • The S&P 500 is expected to finish the year positive, with upward momentum toward the 6,700–6,800 zone by year-end and downside risk to 5,300, but a deep bear market or recession is not the base case.
  • Risk assets should benefit from a benign macro backdrop, modestly easier Fed, and strong tech/AI/semiconductor tailwinds, barring geopolitical shocks.
  • Sector rotation into industrials, U.S. manufacturing, and select commodities may accelerate under heightened tariffs.

Notable Individual Names

  • Intel: Significant U.S. government involvement incoming—expect increased investor scrutiny, possible de-risking of capex plans, and further support for onshoring of chip fabrication.
  • Orsted: Regulatory/capex risk is escalating due to halted U.S. wind projects, with share prices under pressure.
  • Keurig Dr Pepper, JDE Peet’s: Major consolidation wave in coffee/food sector; watch for antitrust or post-merger operational surprises.
  • Apple: Leadership stable, new product innovation cycle (incl. foldables forthcoming) and steady movement into new form factors—core platform remains resilient.
  • Semicap suppliers: Likely to benefit from continued U.S. onshoring and capacity expansion, especially as trade barriers rise.

Risk & Opportunity Watchlist

  • Key Risks: Trade war escalation (tariffs, especially on metals and pharma), geopolitical blow-up in Middle East, regulatory surprises in crypto/tech, sharp cost increases for multinational supply chains.
  • Key Opportunities: U.S. AI/semiconductors, domestic manufacturing, select industrial/defense equities, energy trading strategies (gas > oil), long-term plays on federal infrastructure and tech/industrial policy themes.
