Macro & Policy

Economic Growth & Recession Risk

  • US recession risk for 2025 remains fairly low, with base case of continued positive GDP growth, most likely clustered in the 1–2% range. Likelihood of an economic boom scenario is present but not overwhelming.
  • Unemployment is expected to stay elevated into 2026, with a visible risk of levels around or above 7–8% at some point before 2030—well above recent years, indicating a softer labor market.
  • Despite market strength, corporate bankruptcies are trending high, suggesting not all sectors are recovering, and mid/lower tier companies may face further stress (second order: tightening of credit, possible negative impact on commercial real estate and supplier payment cycles).

Rates & Inflation

  • Multiple indicators point to a moderate downward trajectory for the Fed funds rate in 2025–2026, but no aggressive easing: most likely scenario is 1–2 incremental cuts in 2025.
  • No significant probability of rate hikes or emergency action by the Fed in the next 12 months, supporting modest yield curve normalization and lower rate volatility, especially after mid-2026.
  • Core and headline inflation, while still above pre-pandemic levels, are not expected to spike into crisis territory (>6%); base case has inflation stuck in the 2.5%–3.5% range, indicating persistent stickiness but no near-term reacceleration.
  • Real wage growth should remain slightly positive, preserving consumer spending, but not enough to drive a consumer boom.

Fiscal, Debt & Shutdown Risk

  • US national debt is very likely to surpass $38 trillion by YE 2025 and will remain a major theme for long-duration Treasurys, TIPS, and risk premiums across financial assets.
  • Government spending will increase at least $500B in 2025, but probability for a $1T+ fiscal expansion is below consensus; expect incremental, not drastic, spending shifts.
  • Moderate risk of a government shutdown in late 2025 (high enough to disrupt near-term Treasury/agency bond markets and sentiment).
  • Outlook for further budget rescission actions is mixed, but divided government and political gridlock likely to contain the size of new spending or cuts.

Markets & Sectors

Equities—Broad & Sector

  • Concentration risk remains in place for US mega-cap tech ("Magnificent 7" composition is unlikely to break down this year).
  • Upcoming Nvidia earnings to serve as a bellwether for AI and tech market sentiment.
  • No credible regulatory threat to outright ban Congress from stock trading yet; no near-term structural change to policy-driven returns or market internals.
  • Defensive sectors (consumer staples, utilities) could face SNAP benefit cuts, but odds are mixed—impact may be more psychological (headline risk) than fundamental.

Real Estate

  • Rents are set to continue rising in San Francisco (modestly) and New York (significantly, >5%) in 2025, despite weaker labor market; housing supply constraints and urban demand remain supportive.
  • Western US wildfires (notably Napa) highlight continued physical climate risks to real estate and insurance sectors (affecting both REITs and ag-related names).

Energy, Renewables & Commodities

  • Solar installations: Significant new supply (~40 GW+ feasible), but likely below market's most bullish forecasts; sector growth continues but may disappoint the highest expectations.
  • Clean hydrogen: Catalyst breakthrough news could lower costs and improve the long-term competitiveness of green hydrogen—watch for second order impacts in industrials and energy transition investments.
  • Middle East: Ongoing volatility (Israel-Yemen, Gaza) affects oil risk premium; no extraordinary escalation priced in, but headline risk remains for commodities and defense sectors.

Thematic & Risk Factors

  • Supply chains in flux amid rising US-EU trade frictions and logistics issues—potential cost/pass-through for consumer goods and industrials.
  • Education: Test scores remain a concern; mixed to negative outlook in reading/math. Longer-term implications for workforce quality and competitiveness, but little near-term market impact.
  • Immigration policy is tightening, reducing labor supply in some low-wage sectors—risk to select ag, construction, hospitality, and possibly REIT names in high-immigrant markets.
  • Catastrophe & Disaster Risk: Novel supernova observed and screwworm risk emerging, but immediate market impact limited outside select insurance and ag sectors.

Company- & Asset-Specific

  • Keurig Dr Pepper: Nearing major acquisition, furthering consolidation in the beverage space—look for follow-through in related consumer staples and food/beverage M&A.
  • Nvidia: Imminent earnings will serve as a litmus test for AI-driven growth stories in tech; high volatility expected around results.
  • Watch Solaredge/ENPH for solar sector volatility if installation demand comes below best-case, but fundamentals are still positive.
  • No material US debt default probability for 2025, but Treasury/sovereign risk premiums may remain slightly elevated on political/fiscal headline risk.

Notable Watchpoints Ahead

  • Geopolitical flashpoints (Middle East, US-China/EU tariffs) remain ever-relevant for energy, defense, supply chain, and commodity names.
  • Labor market: Watch for revisions to jobs data and job opening numbers—second order impacts to consumption, housing, REITs, and retail.
  • Fed communications: Tilt is toward policy stability with gentle bias to easing from 2025 onwards, but persistent inflation could delay lower-rate scenarios.
  • Corporate bankruptcies ticking higher—be selective in lower-quality and levered equities/credit, particularly in cyclical industries.

Summary

Near-term outlook is stable for core US equities but with growing clouds at the macro and sectoral level—especially around labor markets, fiscal/debt themes, and policy-driven headline risk. Sectors to overweight: select real estate (urban multifamily, high-rent cities), clean energy/tech leaders (AI, hydrogen, solar at scale). Risks: credit, cyclical equities, commercial real estate, and small caps leaning on cheap debt or facing supply chain/logistics hurdles. Keep a close watch on Fed signaling, government fiscal debates, and geopolitical flare-ups as key catalysts.
