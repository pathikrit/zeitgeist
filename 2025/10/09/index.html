Macro Outlook

Growth and Recession

  • The US is expected to avoid a recession in 2025; GDP growth for the year is likely to be moderate (1.6-2.5%). Strong tail risks for high/low growth outcomes appear discounted. Recession odds rise by 2027 but are not currently elevated.
  • Real wage growth is likely to outpace inflation, supporting consumer activity, though rising unemployment above 4% signals softening in the jobs market.

Inflation, Rates, and Policy

  • Inflation is expected to be sticky, running in the 3–3.5% range in 2025, with core slightly lower but still above the Fed's long-term target.
  • The base case is for at least 3 standard Fed rate cuts in 2025 (75bps total), with little likelihood of hikes or a return to zero rates. Shorter-duration yields will likely drift down; long-duration nominal bonds could see some relief, but the move is capped by fiscal and inflation worries.
  • Fed remains responsive to jobs data, with a modest risk of emergency meetings if labor or housing deteriorate sharply—but not the baseline.

Fiscal and Debt Trajectory

  • Federal deficit and national debt remain on a strong upward path: US debt will almost certainly breach $38T by year-end, reflecting ongoing spending growth with only token likelihood of meaningful new cuts or fiscal discipline.
  • Government spending is set to increase substantially, with the possibility of $500B+ in additional outlays; fiscal consolidation or major cuts (even if political rhetoric intensifies) remain improbable.

Sector and Thematic Views

Bonds and Interest Rate Instruments

  • With multiple rate cuts expected and inflation running modestly above target, US Treasuries may benefit at the short end, but intermediate/long bonds could underperform if fiscal stress or inflation expectations rise. TIPS favorably positioned if inflation remains persistent.
  • Elevated national debt and fiscal deficits could keep curve steepening pressures in play, creating tactical opportunities for duration positioning and inflation-protected strategies.

Equities and Broad Indices

  • The combination of soft-ish but not negative growth, falling rates, and real wage gains support a constructive risk outlook for equities in the near term, though valuation risk (especially in AI-adjacent sectors) is mounting.
  • Policy and liquidity backstop remain supportive, as the Fed is not expected to tighten; watch for renewed risk of volatility if fiscal stress or global shocks flare up.

Technology & Semiconductors

  • The US-China rare earth escalation is likely to create medium-term supply risk for defense and chipmakers; expect volatility and potential for onshoring/reshoring plays (materials, US foundries, specialty metals) to gain renewed attention.
  • AI: Bubble concerns are rising but are not yet consensus; regulatory, margin, and demand risks should be monitored, particularly for mega-cap names.
  • Earnings watch: TSMC guidance will highlight trade/tariff risks and expansion narratives; Google faces regulatory/antitrust and privacy scrutiny alongside strong AI themes.

Industrials, Energy, and Defense

  • China’s export moves bolster the case for defense, aerospace, and onshore manufacturing/processing—potential tailwinds for US industrials and defense suppliers (esp. non-China exposed).
  • Energy and commodity sectors could see secondary effects if continued geopolitical stability leads to lower risk premiums, but remain supported by supply-side constraints.

Consumer and Healthcare

  • Consumer staples likely to flag pricing, inflation, and cost management; names like Procter & Gamble may feel modest margin strain, but underlying demand remains steady.
  • Healthcare/biotech could see opportunistic inflows as flu/bird flu outbreaks return to focus and mask mandates revive defensive interest.

Crypto/Alternatives

  • USDT depeg and other crypto tail risks remain extremely low probability. Potential for renewed focus on digital asset strategies as BlackRock and other financials highlight digital initiatives and private market flows.

Company-Level Highlights

  • BlackRock: Earnings set to emphasize digital assets, private markets, performance/accounting fees, and strong ETF inflows; positive for financial sector flows, especially in alternatives.
  • Tesla: Expect focus on regulatory issues, China exposure, margin/profitability challenges, delivery trends, and emerging tech (Optimus, FSD, Robotaxi). US policy/tariffs or China slowdowns remain watchpoints.
  • Alphabet/Google: AI/DeepMind, Pixel hardware, privacy, and regulatory/antitrust pressures will be key; cybersecurity (Gemini, Wiz) and search monetization trends get attention.
  • TSMC: Watch for updates on US-Japan expansion, rare earth/materials supply risks, and tariff/capacity impacts from China tensions. Automotive, Arizona fab, and AI chip demand remain bright spots.
  • Procter & Gamble: Resilient demand in laundry/beauty, but cost commentary (inflation, tariffs) and advertising spend may pressure margins.
  • Delta Air Lines: High-end leisure and business travel demand remains robust; outlook supports broader consumer resilience narrative.

Second- and Third-Order Watchpoints

  • US-China trade/geopolitical frictions in rare earths may channel to downstream industries—expect US policy incentives for domestic supply chains, and monitor multi-nation corporate pivots away from China.
  • Sticky inflation and rising real wages, combined with moderate monetary policy easing, could fuel a “soft landing” narrative—equities remain supported, but tail risks around policy missteps or fiscal overhang should be respected.
  • Policy response to rising unemployment or housing stress (including possible targeted stimulus or sectoral support) could alter sectoral leadership—particularly in REITs, homebuilders, or consumer credit.
  • Health scares (flu, bird flu, COVID surges) could create temporary risk-off moves or sector rotation back to defensive and healthcare names.

Actionable Summary

  • Favor sectors exposed to US onshoring, supply chain security, and domestic industrial policy.
  • Stay tactically overweight assets benefiting from mild disinflation and Fed rate normalization, with ongoing bias toward quality in both equities and credit.
  • Selectively monitor and adjust for policy-driven volatility around debt ceiling, government spending, and headline labor market weakness.
  • Maintain heightened scenario analysis for further US-China tech/commodity escalation and possible renewed pandemic/virus disruptions.
