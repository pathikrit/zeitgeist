1. Macro & Policy

U.S. Economic Outlook

  • Growth & Labor: The base case for 2025 is moderate GDP growth, within the 1–2.5% range, supporting a "soft landing" scenario. Unemployment is likely to edge up modestly (~4.0–4.2% short term, higher risk by 2030 but not imminent).
  • Inflation: Both headline and core inflation to trend within 2.5–4% for 2025—sticky, but not spiraling. Notably, expectations for core inflation to return below 2.5% are muted, so TIPS outperformance window may be closing.
  • Government Action: Substantial fiscal spending to continue, with new outlays almost certain, sustaining deficits and keeping aggregate demand robust. National debt is on track to stay above $38T, possibly pushing toward $45–$50T by decade’s end. The debt ceiling will be managed (no default scenarios priced).
  • Fed & Rates: Only modest chance of major Fed action this year (emergency meetings or large cuts unlikely), keeping monetary conditions generally stable but with a hawkish undertone.

Regulatory/Legal/Geopolitical

  • Tariffs/Trade: High probability of new tariffs on copper, semiconductors, pharma, and coverage expanding across Asia, EU, and North America, including close allies. Tariff revenue from these policies will be substantial (hundreds of billions).
  • Institutional Risk: Policy volatility (threats to agencies like USAID, regulatory friction) expected. U.S. democracy and institutional checks forecast to erode further, keeping geopolitical and governance risk premia elevated.
  • Currency/FX: USD softness anticipated; de-dollarization efforts bring the USD index to multi-year lows. The EUR/USD pair is seen as volatile; opportunities persist for relative value plays rather than strong directional conviction.
  • EU Cohesion: Probable EU expansion before 2030, but fragmentation pressures linger (e.g., risk of Hungary or Italy voting to leave).

2. Sectors & Asset Classes

Equities & Credit

  • Equities: S&P 500 is consolidating at post-record levels, with low odds of crash-level downside, but also limited upside extension in the near-term. Expect some chop as traders process fiscal/tariff/geopolitical crosscurrents.
  • Labor Market: Wage growth is expected to stay slightly ahead of inflation in 2025, supporting continued but selective consumer strength (especially for discretionaries).
  • Real Estate: Big urban markets face divergent pressures: NYC rents are surging, creating upside for REITs exposed there, while SF is recovering slowly. However, disaster risk in CA (earthquakes, fires) remains a latent threat; insurance and MBS exposed to these regions should be monitored.
  • Credit/Bonds: With deficits persistent and rates stable, long-duration Treasuries may lag, though TIPS appeal is narrowing as inflation risk recedes.

Commodities/Energy

  • Oil: Volatility persists, but downside to $65/bbl is likely in play; upside above $90 is not favored short-term, with SPR levels stable.
  • LNG/Energy Transition: EU’s massive U.S. energy buy is a tailwind for U.S. natural gas and renewables—positive for exporters and select utility operators.
  • Metals: High risk of copper tariffs implies near-term supply chain disruptions and likely spot price volatility; be wary of downstream manufacturing and global trade exposures.
  • Gold/Commodities: Commodity performance may rebalance as USD softens; non-energy commodities potentially benefit from both U.S. fiscal support and new tariff-induced market dislocations.

Technology

  • Semiconductors: U.S. chip production continues to accelerate, Samsung’s new contracts validate momentum. Tariff threats to foreign-made chips reinforce domestic CAPEX/investment, aiding both equipment makers and U.S.-based foundries.
  • Big Tech Antitrust: Ongoing, with serious legal overhang for Amazon and Apple; both face material risk of being ruled monopolies soon. This clouds their ability to deploy capital freely and may prompt restructuring or restrictive settlements.

Climate, Utilities, and Renewables

  • Climate Goals: India is on pace to meet 2030 climate targets; the EU is 50/50, while the U.S. is notably behind. This creates international competitive pressures, trade-related risks for non-compliant sectors, and opportunity for innovative or emerging market investments.
  • Energy Mix: Fossil fuels (oil, coal, gas) will retain dominance through 2030. Renewables growth persists but isn't poised to dethrone conventional sources in this window.

3. Company/Specific Event Highlights

  • UNH & Healthcare: Prepare for volatility as UnitedHealth reports; DOJ scrutiny, cost pressures may pressure stock/sector sentiment.
  • Samsung: Notable U.S. chip deal; expect outperformance vs. global peers.
  • Amazon/Apple: Possible antitrust rulings approaching; be cautious of significant changes to business models or forced spinoffs/splits.
  • Robinhood/AppLovin: Low short-term probability of S&P 500 inclusion—little near-term index flow impact.

4. Emerging/LatAm/Africa

  • LatAm FX: Significant Mexican peso weakening anticipated, as well as a sharp real devaluation—beware of local-currency risks in Brazil and Mexico assets.
  • Political Transitions: Most immediate leadership risk is in Chile (Gabriel Boric) and Peru (Dina Boluarte). Elevated political risks across Africa, particularly Nigeria and DRC.
  • India: Remains a rare climate and economic reform bright spot among large emerging markets.

5. Other Notable Themes

  • Disaster Risk: Catastrophic events (supervolcano, magnitude 8+ earthquakes) remain tail risks but with low probabilities in coming years—insurance remains underpriced for extreme events.
  • Crypto/Stablecoins: Tether depeg risk is present but not imminent; maintain caution on stablecoin-adjacent exposures as regulatory and technical scrutiny rises.
  • Space/Innovation: Despite SpaceX Crew-11 progress, human Mars missions before 2030 are a long shot, so limit near-term speculation.

Summary Actions for Investors

  • Maintain a pro-risk tilt, but be selective: favor U.S. LNG, renewable infrastructure, and domestic semiconductor plays.
  • Use USD weakness to inform multi-asset, international allocations and commodity exposures.
  • Monitor big tech legal risk and supply chain disruptions from tariffs; trim excesses where policy shocks are concentrated.
  • Home in on urban REITs in NYC and the Southeast, but remain disciplined on CA risk.
  • Stay alert to near-term market volatility—spikes could produce tactical buying opportunities without broader crash signals yet manifesting.
  • Continue to hedge disaster and policy risks through sector and geography, not just index-wide insurance.
