1. MACRO OVERVIEW

US Economic Outlook & Policy

  • GDP Growth: Growth is likely to remain moderate, centered around 1.5-2.5% in 2025. A sharp economic boom appears unlikely despite campaign rhetoric; significant acceleration would be unexpected barring major policy shifts.
  • Inflation: Core and headline inflation are seen remaining moderately elevated (mostly 3-3.5%), with a strong consensus against inflation spiking beyond 4%.
  • Wages: Wages are expected to outpace inflation over 2025, supporting consumer purchasing power and retail sectors, but cost pressures on businesses, particularly SMEs, will persist.
  • Unemployment & Jobs: Unemployment is expected to trend slightly higher (above 4%), with job creation slowing—implications for cyclical stocks, housing, and consumer lending.
  • Government Spending & Debt: Major, ongoing federal spending increases are expected, pushing US debt past $38 trillion this year. Higher deficits could sustain upward pressure on long-term yields and intensify future debates about tax and spending.

Federal Reserve & Interest Rates

  • Rate Path: Markets see a strong likelihood of multiple rate cuts (up to three) in 2025, with the Fed funds rate drifting below 3.5% by 2026. A rate hike or emergency rate cut is seen as unlikely.
  • Treasury Yields: Limited probability is assigned to 10-year yields exceeding 4.6% this year, which suggests downward pressure on yields—primarily driven by recession concerns, Fed easing, and global capital flows into Treasuries as risk perceptions rise due to tariffs and geopolitical noise.

2. GEOPOLITICS, TRADE & POLICY RISKS

US-China and Global Tariffs

  • The US is poised to pursue widespread tariff increases in 2025–2026, especially targeting China, India, South Korea, Taiwan, Japan, Mexico, Canada, and the EU. Tariff rates against China could rise into the 20-40% range.
    • Second-order effects: Supply chain diversification is accelerating (SE Asia, Mexico, nearshoring). Manufacturing costs are likely to rise for complex goods (tech, industrial, autos), and input inflation is a real risk. Gross margins for exporters/importers and multinationals will be pressured.
    • Tech, Pharma & Semiconductors: Tech and pharma likely face new tariffs, with semiconductor-specific levies almost even odds. Watch for sector volatility, regulatory scrutiny, and shifting investment patterns.
    • Consumer impact/Inflation: Tariffs risk adding price pressure on consumer goods but may not immediately change headline CPI due to moderating demand.

Immigration, Regulation, and Political Uncertainty

  • Restrictive H-1B visa policies (i.e., $100,000 fees) could slow US growth, especially in tech and healthcare, push more R&D offshore, and crimp STEM labor supply.
  • Political and policy volatility (court actions, unexpected regulation, trade probes) are expected, supporting structurally higher risk premia for US indices.
  • Growing global attention on Palestine, with Israel signaling potential harsh retaliation. Mideast/EU risk premiums may rise (energy, defense sectors).

3. SECTOR & THEMATIC TRENDS

Energy & Commodities

  • Primary Energy Mix: Oil and coal will remain the largest contributors to global energy in 2030; the pace of solar and wind penetration is solid but insufficient to materially displace hydrocarbons in this horizon.
    • Expect steady demand for oil & gas equipment/services; coal asset write-downs remain a long-term risk.
  • Gasoline Prices: CA gasoline prices demonstrate volatility, exposing refiners, consumer staples/retailers, and transport sectors to headline risk.
  • Renewables: US solar installation is set to continue robustly (30-40GW in 2025), but massive exponential growth is not anticipated yet. Opportunity in grid upgrades and storage more than in dramatic panel expansion.

Industrial & Manufacturing

  • Sectors with high import inputs remain exposed to tariffs, supply chain disruptions, and policy swing risk. Companies with local manufacturing/flexible sourcing will fare better.

Healthcare & Pharma

  • Policy-driven volatility: New pharma tariffs, ongoing regulatory narratives, and high-profile acquisitions (e.g., Pfizer-Metsera boosting focus on obesity/GLP-1 pipeline).
  • Unusual risk in OTC drugs and major branded pharma related to government publicity (e.g., Tylenol-autism link discussion could trigger litigation and product liability drag on major consumer healthcare conglomerates).

Tech & AI

  • China, South Korea, and Taiwan-specific tariffs/tech policies pressure tech profitability (hardware, chips, gadgets, electronics, and certain app platforms facing possible scrutiny or restrictions).
  • US and global semiconductor production growth is expected to be substantial (>7% in 2025); see above-average momentum for domestic chipmakers and fab-related capital investments.
  • AI infrastructure arms race: Nvidia's $100B planned investment in OpenAI is a significant acceleration of compute spend and signals ultra-high capex from cloud, tech, and hyperscaler buyers. Second-order: memory, datacenter REITs, advanced packaging and programming infrastructure will benefit.

5. CRYPTO & ALTERNATIVES

  • Stablecoins (USDC, Tether) have low, but non-negligible, risk of de-pegging; watch regulatory developments and contagion risks to DeFi and crypto liquidity.
  • Bitcoin is unlikely to reach hyper-bullish price targets (>$200k), but medium-term upside to the $130-150k range is seen as a toss-up—momentum sensitive to global risk appetite, monetary easing cycles, and regulatory direction.

SUMMARY & TACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS

  • Expect higher US government spending and debt, with muted GDP acceleration and sticky but not runaway inflation. Federal Reserve rate cuts seem likely, which will support longer duration assets and compress yields.
  • Trade/tariff escalation and labor/migration curbs are structural risks for global supply chains, corporates with int’l exposure, and sectors dependent on skilled labor (tech, biotech, pharma).
  • Ongoing AI and semiconductor investment themes accelerate, especially favoring US-based chip supply chain, AI hardware, and aligned datacenter providers.
  • Healthcare sees regulatory flux and reputational risk, while consumer and industrials must navigate tariff and margin swings.
  • Watch for volatility but not necessarily crisis in equity indices; circuit breaker risk, while present, remains a tail event for now.
  • Remain nimble on sector/factor exposure, favoring areas with pricing power, domestic tailwinds, and strategic ties to the AI/semiconductor infrastructure cycle. Defensive positioning in bonds/alternatives becomes more attractive if economic risks crystallize or if policy volatility further rises.
