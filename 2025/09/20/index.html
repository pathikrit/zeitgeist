1. Macroeconomic Environment

US Growth, Inflation & Fed Policy

  • Growth: US GDP growth for 2025 will likely be moderate (1.5-2.5%). Recession risk is viewed as low. Odds for a "Trump boom" (5%+ GDP) are limited.
  • Inflation: Both headline and core inflation are firmly expected above 3% for 2025. Any rapid drop below 2.5% is highly unlikely.
  • Labor Market: Unemployment is expected to climb throughout the decade—7%+ is the base case, raising risk for sectors reliant on flexible/cheap labor or consumer confidence.
  • Rates: The current trajectory points to multiple (up to 3) Fed rate cuts over the next 12-18 months, with rates settling in the 3.5%-3.75% range. No material risk of hikes or emergency rate policy changes.

Implications:

  • Short and intermediate-duration US bonds regain relative appeal as inflation remains stickier than target but rate cycle softens.
  • Persistent inflation could keep real yields suppressed and support for hard assets (commodities, gold, real estate).

Fiscal Outlook & Debt

  • Federal Debt: Strong consensus that debt will cross $38T by year-end, trending toward $40T+ under the Trump administration.
  • Spending: Increased spending (>$500B) is highly likely, with potential for “fiscal cliffs” around shutdowns or entitlement reform.

Implications:

  • Long-term US sovereign risk rises modestly. Slight elevation in credit risk, but no imminent crisis.
  • Watch for potential downside in US Treasuries if debt trajectory triggers new credit downgrades or sparks bond market dislocation.

2. Policy, Trade, and Regulation

Trade & Tariffs

  • Broad and sweeping US tariff increases are extremely likely across all major trading partners (China, Canada, Mexico, Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, plus parts of the EU).
  • Pharma and semiconductor sectors are specifically highlighted for near-term new tariffs.
  • Large increases in US tariff revenue are likely.
  • H-1B visa fee shock and broader anti-immigration stance will stress US tech labor supply and cost structures.

Implications:

  • Near-term negative for US importers (retail, consumer hardware, auto), as input costs rise.
  • US tech companies exposed to supply chains in China/India especially vulnerable, with labor access further constrained.
  • Pharma faces direct cost and supply chain shocks.
  • Potential for retaliatory tariffs, pressuring ag, autos, and heavy US exporters.
  • Ensuing sectors to benefit: domestic manufacturing, supply chain “localization” enablers, select automation, and robotics.

Social Policy & Benefits

  • Social Security reform, especially raising retirement age, now under active consideration; a policy inflection could dampen consumer confidence among 50+ age group longer term.

3. Sector & Thematic Focus

Technology, Semiconductors & AI

  • Nvidia is strongly favored to become the world’s largest public company, underpinned by US semiconductor output growth (>7%).
  • Microsoft, Apple, Alphabet, Tesla: None are likely to overtake Nvidia in valuation in near-term.
  • AI regulation: US legislative action on AI safety is considered probable this year.
  • Labor supply shocks: H-1B cracks and tariff cost-push will compound wage and margin pressures, particularly for large tech.
  • Crypto & Blockchain: Strong outlook for Bitcoin (very likely to cross $130K) and Ripple. Ethereum downside is limited (sub-$1,000 risk is minimal).

Implications:

  • Remain overweight US semiconductor capital equipment, design, and enabling AI infrastructure.
  • US mega-cap tech faces risk/volatility around labor and trade (especially those with India/China exposure).
  • Tariff inflation on consumer gadgets (see Xbox price hikes) hints at potential cost pass-through across hardware names.

Healthcare & Pharma

  • High likelihood of direct US pharma sector tariffs.
    • US pharma MNCs: Margin pressure, supply chain noise; benefit to domestic generics and US API manufacturers.
    • Healthcare providers: Some input cost pressure, but mostly a manufacturer/importer issue.
  • Disease flags: polio, “kissing bug,” flesh-eating bacteria—no systemic threat yet, but risk monitoring is warranted for biotech and medtech diagnostics.

Energy & Climate

  • Solar: 2025’s US installation rate will be strong (30-40 GW), but not at the upper end of expectations; no new upside momentum indicated.
  • Wind: Offshore wind development faces new regulatory and permitting headwinds (revoked permits).
  • Fossil/Utilities: No sector-specific news, but continued renewables policy uncertainty favors balance in energy holdings.

Implications:

  • Maintain a measured approach to renewables and energy transition equities; overweight US traditional utilities and infrastructure during policy flux.

Retail, Real Estate, Consumer:

  • Walmart faces probes on third-party sales quality—potential for short- and medium-term operational or regulatory drag.
  • Persistent consumer inflation pressure, and possible entitlements/benefits tightening, will weigh on broadline retail and discretionary, though employment remains stable enough to avoid severe downside.
  • Government shutdown risk may affect sector sentiment.

Aerospace & Defense:

  • SpaceX: Robust launch schedule continues, signaling strong underlying demand for US launch vehicles.
