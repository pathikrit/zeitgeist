  • Nasdaq rose, dollar fell as markets weigh shutdown risk. Wall Street bracing for disruption in economic data if shutdown proceeds.
  • Netanyahu reportedly agrees to Trump plan for Gaza, but Hamas compliance uncertain; Trump eyes Gaza ‘peace board.’
  • Reports of potential new U.S. arms support for Ukraine; Russia threatens escalation.
  • Afghanistan suffers nationwide internet blackout under Taliban.

  • Apple supply chain: Samsung preparing for iPhone Fold display production, foldable iPhone rumors heating up.
  • Starbucks closes multiple stores as part of turnaround plan.
  • Qualcomm announces AI chips outperforming Apple/Intel/AMD benchmarks.
  • Florida expected to ban mRNA vaccine mandates.

Macroeconomic & Policy Outlook

Monetary Policy & Rates

  • Federal Reserve is highly unlikely to hike rates or make emergency cuts in 2025. Instead, the base case is for a moderate rate cut cycle, with multiple (but not aggressive) rate reductions expected. This stance is consistent with persistent, but mostly contained, inflation and no major credit or liquidity event on the horizon.
  • Inflation above 3% is almost a given for 2025, but rapid acceleration past 4% is considered a tail risk. Treasury yields are expected to trend below 4% during the year but remain above historic crisis lows, signaling ongoing restrictive conditions but not a deflationary scenario.
  • U.S. national debt pushing past $38T is almost certain, but aggressive rate or fiscal policy responses are unlikely near-term.

Implications:

  • Growth stocks’ risk/reward profile remains favorable versus deep value, as prolonged high rates (5%+) are not expected.
  • Short/intermediate-dated Treasuries and TIPS see some support from declining yields but not a disinflationary windfall.
  • Favor portfolios positioned for modest rate declines, steady but elevated inflation, and muted volatility events.

Fiscal Policy & Shutdown

  • Government shutdown in 2025 now appears almost certain. Immediate impact will be economic data delays, some consumer spending drag, and possibly contracting federal-related sectors (defense, contractors).
  • Nearly half of DoD civilians will be furloughed—temporary uncertainty for defense names, contractors, and regional economies with large federal footprints.
  • Tax and immigration policy changes (cuts or large-scale deportations) remain very unlikely in the short-term, limiting the chance of major sectoral disruption or boost.

Implications:

  • Any prolonged shutdown poses headline risk and could delay market data, weighing on near-term equity volatility.
  • Second and third-order risks for small-cap contractors and financials closely tied to government activity.
  • Validate any sector overweights for government-related income streams or regulatory catalysts.

Geopolitical Landscape

Ukraine, Russia, and Commodities

  • No near-term expectation for a Russia–Ukraine ceasefire or leadership changes in Russia. Escalatory risk persists but not expected to fundamentally disrupt commodity flows or currencies.
  • Trump administration potential support for Ukraine with advanced weapons is now publicly floated, but Russian escalation remains a core risk scenario.
  • Middle East volatility (Netanyahu, Gaza) mostly contained as of now, with new peace initiatives unlikely to deliver meaningful stability or energy price shocks short-term.

Implications:

  • Energy complex remains capped by supply discipline rather than acute disruption.
  • No durable relief in risk premium for European or U.S. defense and energy exposures.
  • Remain vigilant for tail risks, but base case is status quo.

Other

  • Moldovan elections indicate continued EU alignment, minimal immediate impact unless Russian pressure escalates.

Sector & Theme Notes

Technology & AI

  • Significant AI hardware innovation continues (Qualcomm advances in AI chips versus Apple/Intel). Even so, “disruption” to big incumbents is unlikely in the near term—no dominant new AI model is expected beyond prevailing leaders.
  • OpenAI, Meta, and Anthropic are not viewed as near-term threats to the Nvidia/Big Tech status quo in AI platform leadership.
  • Foldable iPhone looks increasingly likely (Samsung prepping production), but launch not expected within 2025, so current Apple cycle dynamics unchanged this year.

Crypto & Digital Assets

  • USDT depeg risk remains very low, with broad crypto market stability expected.
  • Bitcoin is not expected to see extreme moves to the upside (e.g., $250K+) but moderate new highs ($130K+) viewed as plausible. Ethereum shows more momentum potential ($5K+ achievable, deeper dips less likely).

Consumer & Health

  • Weed rescheduling remains a coin toss, but not enough momentum for baseline legislative change in 2025. Medical/rec weed sector faces ongoing uncertainty.
  • mRNA vaccine mandate bans (e.g. Florida) could ripple as regional precedents but are unlikely to trigger nationwide health sector realignment.
  • Starbucks and other large consumer chains closing locations reflects micro-level cost pressures rather than macro crisis; minimal sector-wide read-across.

Special Situations

  • Major M&A: EA buyout is industry-defining, may drive further gaming/content deal speculation.
  • Media: Washington Post sale possible, but not yet likely, so no clear sector-wide trigger.
  • DOJ/antitrust risks to Meta/Big Tech not expected to culminate this year (forced asset sales or settlements unlikely in 2025).

Company & Name-Specific

  • Apple: No foldable launch in 2025, but supply chain investments and Samsung involvement are constructive for long-term hardware refresh hopes.
  • Nvidia: No near-term AI model leadership risk; hardware remains entrenched.
  • Meta: No forced sale or major settlement this year; regulatory headwinds persist but not disruptive.
  • Uber/Waymo: Waymo not expected to overtake Uber in San Francisco in 2025.
  • MicroStrategy: Not expected to face a Bitcoin margin call/liquidation event.
  • JPMorgan/EA: $55B Electronic Arts take-private is the biggest headline; focus on private equity appetite for content assets.

Bottom Line

  • The dominant macro narrative for U.S. markets is a slow, uneven normalization: policy remains restrictive but not panicked, modest inflation (3–4%), contained volatility, and no game-changing fiscal/tax reforms.
  • Government shutdown injects uncertainty but not acute systemic risk; prepare for tactical volatility and economic data gaps.
  • No major shifts expected in global risk landscape—Ukraine, Russia, and Middle East status largely persist.
  • Tech/AI hype continues but fundamental leadership remains unchanged in the near term.
  • Remain overweight quality growth, cautious on government-dependent cyclicals, and selective in commodity and digital assets exposure. Continue to monitor for idiosyncratic event risk, especially in tech (hardware, M&A) and political standoffs.
