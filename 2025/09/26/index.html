Macro Overview

Fiscal Policy, Recession, and Rates

  • US Fiscal Outlook:
    • Accelerated federal spending likely in 2025; high confidence in at least $500B increase, supporting continued deficit growth.
    • US National Debt widely expected to surpass $38T this year (near consensus). Debt-to-GDP ratios will stay below crisis territory for now but will increase market sensitivity to future rate spikes or policy changes.
  • Recession & Growth:
    • Market assigns moderate probability to US or global recession before 2027; while base case remains continued growth, recession risks cannot be ignored.
    • GDP growth for 2025 is most likely in the middling 1.6%-2.5% range, with upside or downside surprises not entirely off the table.
  • Inflation/Rates:
    • Both headline and core inflation are expected to remain sticky, clustering mostly in a 3-3.5% band through 2025, above the Fed’s target and suggesting persistence in price pressures.
    • Wage growth is likely to continue exceeding inflation, with housing (rents) as a persistent driver of core CPI.
    • Rate cuts: High probability of at least three rate cuts by the Fed in 2025, marking a shift to easing bias, but ultra-dovish surprise (emergency cuts or rate hikes) appears low-risk.
  • Second-order Effects:
    • Higher nominal rates, sustained wage pressures, and deficit spending underpin stagflationary risks but don’t signal an outright recession; positive backdrop for select equities, commodities with pricing power, and inflation hedges.
    • Treasuries and TIPS to benefit from rate normalization and cut expectations, but rising debt and fiscal expansion will raise tail-risk premia.
    • Uncertainty from the election cycle could create episodic volatility in macro assets and sectors sensitive to defense, healthcare, and US-China relations.

Sector and Thematic Insights

Trade, Tariffs, and Geopolitics

  • Tariff Regime:
    • Trump seen as very likely to implement sweeping tariffs (notably 30-40%+) on China, Taiwan, Mexico, the EU, Japan, South Korea, and Canada—with near-term action on healthcare/pharma and semiconductors.
    • Second/third-order effects:
      • Upward pressure on US consumer and producer inflation, particularly for goods and supply-chain-exposed categories.
      • Chronic headwinds for multinationals with significant non-US manufacturing; supply chain reorientation (nearshoring, Mexico, Southeast Asia) to benefit industrial landlords, logistics, and select US manufacturers.
      • Export-driven countries (Germany, Japan, SK, Taiwan) and firms in autos, chips, pharmaceuticals disproportionately exposed.
  • China, Emerging Markets:
    • Direct negative for global growth, especially for EM exporters. Turkey risk is elevated with Erdoğan predicted to continue to dominate politics.
    • Lower commodity prices (oil, soybeans) signal sluggish global demand, weighing on EM assets—although Argentina/China disrupting US agriculture exports also lowers the floor for US farm incomes.

Energy/Commodities

  • Fossil Fuels:
    • Consensus points to weak oil and natural gas prices (<$65 WTI; muted drawdown risk for natgas this winter), likely due to tepid global demand offsetting supply risks.
    • This is a net negative for energy sector profits, export-dependent economies, and inflation hedges tied to oil.
  • Renewables and Energy Transition:
    • US solar installations expected to continue robust growth (especially above 30GW), consistent with federal support, but not at blowout levels; modest windfall for utilities exposed to renewables, but not a sector game-changer for 2025.
    • Substantial probability of direct US investment in lithium supply (notably Lithium Americas), reinforcing long-term EV/battery supply chain resilience. This could prompt re-rating of US battery, EV, and lithium mining equities.
    • Second-order: Likely more regulatory and capital inflows into US-centric supply chains, with select support for industrials, mining, and renewables.

Real Estate and Housing

  • Rent and Property Trends:
    • Substantial rental price increases expected in NYC and San Francisco (NYC >6%, SF >2%); residential REITs and property owners may outperform versus inflation in these metro markets.
    • Elevated rents further entrench consumer inflation, supporting the case for inflation-protected assets.
    • NYC mobility still below pre-pandemic levels; mixed read-through for office and retail REITs, but housing demand remains solid.

Alternatives & Crypto

  • Gold vs Crypto:
    • Gold expected to materially outperform Bitcoin in 2025; continued macro risk, sticky inflation, and potential for risk-off flows underscore the attractiveness of gold over more speculative alternatives in the near term.
    • However, long-term and corporate adoption narratives for Bitcoin remain alive (another S&P 500 company is moderately likely to add BTC to its balance sheet by 2026).
  • Crypto Trends:
    • Bitcoin and Ethereum have strong support above current levels, but probability for extreme upside ($150K+ BTC, $10K ETH) remains modest; more moderate rallies are favored.
    • Major airdrops (Monad, MegaETH) anticipated into 2026, with DeFi protocols trending higher. Solana's all-time high is plausible, but not consensus.
    • Regulatory uncertainty remains, especially regarding national reserves or government-level crypto policy.

Individual/Company-specific Notes

  • Tech:
    • Google and Apple leadership turnover risk negligible for 2025; company strategy continuity likely.
    • TikTok US business likely to be stabilized under American stewardship (Oracle, Silver Lake, MGX), removing a key overhang for US social media/advertising incumbents.
    • Amazon faces short-term reputational and financial impact from FTC settlement, but long-term business franchise resiliency remains.
    • Starbucks is restructuring with targeted closures and layoffs—watch for continued cost optimization and how management navigates margin pressure.
  • Healthcare/Pharma:
    • Drug tariffs increasingly likely and could spill into higher healthcare costs; expect margin pressure for US-based pharma importers or companies dependent on non-US supply chains.
    • Milestones in gene therapies and cheap HIV prophylaxis may drive sector rotation and positive sentiment in select biotech names, especially those with strong regulatory or IP barriers.

Second & Third Order Considerations

  • Trade escalation and tariffs may re-anchor inflation higher in late 2025, challenge global earnings forecasts, and create differentiated sector winners/losers.
  • Increased US fiscal spending and infrastructure investments could offset macro drag for capital goods, certain tech, and construction industries.
  • Rent inflation creates a stickier overall CPI; sustained wage increases may shift consumer behavior, helping discretionary names with pricing power but hurting those exposed to lower-income spenders.
  • Persistent global and domestic political risk warrants higher hedging (gold, TIPS, select defensives) and focuses attention on “home-shoring” and domestic supply chains.
  • Geopolitical and security risks around energy, food supply, and tech platforms will continue to drive regulatory and market volatility.
