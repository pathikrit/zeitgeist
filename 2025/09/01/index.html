Daily Memo (01-Sep-2025)

Key News Highlights

  • Afghanistan Earthquake: Over 800 deaths reported, possible humanitarian crisis with aid requests to the international community.
  • US Federal Appeals Court Overturns Tariffs: Legal setback for Trump's trade policy, with potential Supreme Court implications. Allies express confusion.
  • Potential US Government Shutdown: Political standoff brewing as Congress reconvenes after recess, raising fiscal uncertainty.
  • Union Pressure and Labor Policy: Media reports Trump's moves to weaken public-sector unions, stoking domestic labor tensions.
  • India-Russia Energy Ties: Modi and Putin strengthen energy cooperation, countering US positions; attended by Xi, signaling a shift in global alliances.
  • China Counters US Policy: Xi criticizes US "bullying," promotes a China-centered global order alongside Russia and India.
  • Russia Suspected of GPS Jamming: Alleged interference with EU leaders' flights raises geopolitical risk.
  • Tourism Declines in US: Ongoing deterioration in foreign tourism blamed on US policies, affecting travel and hospitality sectors.
  • Pending Jobs Report, Corporate Earnings: Markets await job data and earnings (Broadcom spotlight), expected to drive near-term volatility.

Macro & Policy

Economic Growth, Recession, and Rates

  • Moderate US Growth Outlook: GDP growth for 2025 is widely expected to be sluggish, most likely around or under the low 2% range. Prospects for a significant "Trump boom" are being discussed, but consensus expects only modest outperformance.
  • Soft Landing but High Uncertainty: The risk of a US recession starting before 2027 remains present, though not the base case. Near-term, labor market indicators and upcoming jobs data will be watched closely for early warning signs.
  • Rates Path: Most scenarios do not foresee aggressive Fed hikes, and the expectation is for only a handful of rate cuts in 2025 (likely 1–2). Elevated but range-bound federal funds rate (>3.25% but <3.75%) is likely, which should have a cooling effect on housing and credit availability, but not trigger a credit crunch.
  • Inflation Persistence: Both headline and core CPI are likely to stay elevated, predominantly in the low-to-mid 3% range. Notably, markets see it as virtually certain that inflation will stay above 3%—well above the Fed’s target. Markets do not anticipate a high likelihood of a sudden inflation spike (>6%), but sticky price pressures will likely remain a theme impacting consumer and wage negotiations.

Fiscal & Federal Spending

  • Debt Trajectory: The US national debt continues to climb, plausibly exceeding $38 trillion by year-end and moving toward $40 trillion or higher under Trump’s term. Meaningful fiscal consolidation (deep spending cuts) is not expected, even with renewed political focus.
  • Government Shutdown Risk: High headline risk as Congress reconvenes, with a government shutdown widely discussed.
  • Tax Policy: No broad-based tax cuts for high earners are expected, minimizing major demand-side fiscal surprises.
  • Trade/Tariffs: Legal rulings weakening Trump-era tariffs create additional uncertainty for global supply chains; these may feed into both cost structures and currency volatility.
  • Union and Labor Policy: Weakening of union strength could tamp down wage growth pressure, with indirect impact on consumption.

Geopolitics

  • US-China-India-Russia Axis: Increasing energy and strategic cooperation among India, China, and Russia suggests further bifurcation of global supply chains, and may sustain the current demand for fossil fuels and non-US dollar reserves.
  • Military Risks: Low but non-negligible likelihoods of military conflict in East Asia (China-Taiwan, China-Philippines), as well as Russia-related risk in Europe. First-order effects are still muted but defense sector resilience and energy hedging remain prudent.

Sector Implications

Energy & Commodities

  • Fossil Fuels Endure: Oil and coal are likely to remain the world’s largest sources of primary energy through 2030—expectation of a swift renewables takeover is unsupported by consensus. India-Russia ties and US policy shifts only reinforce that status quo.
  • Oil Prices: High volatility: There is significant two-way risk on crude, with meaningful probability of both a downward swing (sub-$65 WTI) and the potential for spikes above $80 if shocks (e.g., geopolitical) occur. For now, the base case is subdued.
  • Natural Gas: High likelihood of price rebounds above $4–$5 in 2025, which may benefit US producers and international exporters.
  • Renewables: US solar capacity is likely to continue expanding, but not at an exponential (hypergrowth) rate. Renewables stocks may remain sentiment-driven, with policy catalysts lacking.
  • Gold/Commodities: Elevated inflation expectations support a role for inflation hedges.

Financials & Real Estate

  • Yields: Treasury yields unlikely to see sharp spikes above 5% near-term, reflecting stable if elevated inflation and rates.
  • Housing/Rents: Rising rents in key urban centers—particularly New York—favors REITs and urban property holders. San Francisco growth remains positive but less pronounced.
  • Government Shutdown/Spending Uncertainty: May create near-term volatility for financials with high US exposure.

Technology

  • Market Leadership: Big Tech remains dominant; there is no indication that S&P 500's concentration in the "Magnificent 7" will dissipate in 2025. Niche sectors (AI, semiconductors) continue to drive index performance.
  • AI/Semiconductors: Nvidia appears favored to remain the world's largest company by year-end, strongly benefitting from AI and high-performance compute tailwinds. Microsoft, Apple, Google, and Tesla are long shots for dethroning Nvidia in market cap.
  • Crypto: Institutional adoption of Bitcoin continues, with significant likelihood of further treasury allocation from major S&P 500 firms. However, no consensus on a "moonshot" for Bitcoin or Ethereum (e.g., >$150k BTC, >$8k ETH) by year-end, with more moderate upside in play.
  • Mobility: No major disruption to Uber’s SF market share by Waymo is anticipated in the short term.

Healthcare & Consumer

  • Healthcare Policy: Momentum in rescheduling cannabis reflects a shifting regulatory environment but not a consensus move yet.
  • Pharmaceuticals: Political scrutiny over drug pricing is on the rise, with direct statements from the White House. Expect defensive posturing from drugmakers.
  • Consumer Strength: Despite inflation, wage growth is expected to at least keep pace, supporting consumption, but squeezed margins may persist in low-end retail and services sectors.

Individual Companies

  • Nvidia: Strong prospects as global market leader; AI/semiconductor tailwinds remain exceptionally robust.
  • Microsoft, Apple, Alphabet, Tesla: Less likely to lead market cap rankings in 2025; may underperform expectations if sentiment rotates out of ultra-megacap tech.
  • Uber/Waymo: Incumbency remains strong for Uber in mobility; no major disruption from autonomous entrants expected this year.
  • Cannabis (Tilray, MSOS): Optionality on rescheduling, but catalyst not assured. Monitor for further federal or state-level signals.
