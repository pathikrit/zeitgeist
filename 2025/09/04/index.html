Macro & Economic Outlook

Growth, Labor, and Inflation

  • Growth: US GDP growth for 2025 is likely to be positive, but modest—expectation centers near 1.5–2.5%. Risk of outright recession or negative quarterly GDP is low, but not zero. Employment data points to higher unemployment rates, trending towards or above 4.1% by Sep 2025, and subdued monthly jobs growth.
  • Inflation: Persistent inflation remains a consensus for 2025, with general and core inflation rates widely expected to stay above 3%. The probability of inflation exceeding 5% is low but not negligible; upside risks (commodity supply, fiscal/monetary surprises) linger.
  • Monetary Policy: Markets firmly expect the Fed to cut rates at least once, with a meaningful chance of 2 cuts, but aggressive or emergency easing looks unlikely unless growth falters sharply. Yield curve steepening or volatility in response to dovish surprise is possible. No rate hikes expected through 2025.

Implications

  • Anticipate monetary conditions to ease moderately. This may support risk asset multiples, especially interest-sensitive sectors (tech, REITs, higher-yield debt) over longer duration.
  • Bonds: Intermediate and longer-dated Treasuries may see some capital gains as yields gradually drift lower, but the probability of a dramatic yield collapse (to sub-2%) is considered very low.
  • Real assets (commodities, gold) likely maintain value as inflation persists. Stagflation and deep recession tail risks remain contained.

Sector Themes

Technology & Semiconductors

  • US semiconductor production is poised for strong double-digit growth in 2025, driven by reshoring policies and persistent high demand. Tech sector, especially AI and compute infrastructure, maintains favorable long-term momentum despite headline layoff risk.
  • AI Adoption: Large-scale AI adoption (e.g., ChatGPT’s user base) continues rapidly, with further monetization and platform ecosystem opportunities for hardware (chips) and cloud/software names.
  • Antitrust: Most Big Tech antitrust overhangs are unlikely to result in forced divestitures this year, but regulatory/legal noise will persist and episodically impact market leaders (Google, Meta, Apple).
  • Layoffs: Tech sector layoffs exceeding 2024 numbers are expected. Short-term headwinds for labor/office markets and some SaaS providers, but overall sector margin discipline may bolster earnings growth for larger players.

Financials, Real Estate, and Insurance

  • Low-probability but high-impact risks: Major earthquakes in California or Japan remain non-negligible tail events, which bears monitoring for insurance underwriters, catastrophe bonds, and the West Coast property market.
  • Market structure risk: NYSE “circuit breaker” event has very low odds, but would signal acute liquidity distress if triggered—stay alert for signs of market-wide stress.

Healthcare & Policy

  • Vaccine policy fragmentation: Growing state-federal conflict over vaccine mandates and RFK’s move to reconstitute CDC panels indicate high policy uncertainty. This opens medium-term volatility for vaccine makers, life sciences, and the healthcare sector.
  • Broader public health: Emerging diseases (Chagas, rising cancer rates) could drive renewed investment in diagnostics, treatments, and health services.

Energy & Renewables

  • US solar buildout: Installations in 2025 will likely be robust, but targets above 50 GWdc look out of reach; ongoing sector growth, though not at bursting-optimism levels. Solar and utility-scale renewables remain a strong long-term investment case, but room for near-term disappointment relative to most aggressive forecasts.

Crypto & Alternative Assets

  • Bitcoin and crypto assets: Downside risk for Bitcoin below $50k is very low, while Ethereum continues a robust upward trajectory with ETF approvals for Ripple, Dogecoin, Litecoin, and Cardano highly likely over the next year. Crypto’s “mainstreaming” in ETF wrappers continues.
  • MicroStrategy: Margin calls or forced liquidations are highly unlikely unless crypto prices collapse dramatically.
  • Trump digital assets: Political volatility and legal outcomes continue to affect derivative meme-crypto instruments.

Geopolitical Risks & Policy

  • US government shutdown risk is elevated and a coin flip; episodic volatility for contractors/government-heavy sectors is possible.
  • Tariffs and trade: Trump’s appeal to save tariffs and open discussion of “unwinding” trade deals indicate rising trade and policy uncertainty, with possible implications for global supply chains, domestic inflation, and multinationals (Apple directly in focus).
  • Ukraine, NATO, and Middle East: Probability of significant NATO/EU troop escalation in Ukraine or major NATO instability is low, but persistent regional risk premium remains, especially for energy and defense stocks.
  • China: Military parade demonstrates further advances in domestic technology—expect continued US-China strategic decoupling and arms race, especially for defense, AI, and semiconductor sectors.

Key Company/Name Watch

  • Apple: Facing incremental cost and pricing pressures from tariffs and supply chain issues. Upside from AI software upgrades, but hardware margin headwinds persist.
  • Google/Alphabet: Antitrust regulatory risk muted for now after US court victory, though EU/other actions may still pose headlines.
  • Meta: Forced spinning off of WhatsApp or Instagram currently very improbable.
  • Figma: Recent post-IPO volatility and upcoming share unlock pressure; valuation recalibration for new software IPOs may spill over to peers.
  • American Eagle: Positive retail momentum highlights the power of effective marketing and influencer tie-ins—sentiment could spill over to consumer discretionary.
  • MicroStrategy ($MSTR): Leverage to Bitcoin remains a core risk, but balance sheet stress unlikely barring severe crypto drawdown.

Second-Order Considerations

  • A government shutdown could interact with ongoing healthcare, trade, and tech policy uncertainty to amplify volatility in late 2025.
  • Persistent elevated inflation likely means wage and cost pressures, supporting union/labor action (“push-pull” for services sector margin and retail sales), while also underpinning physical assets and commodities valuations.
  • An incremental firming of US-China rivalry across tech, AI, and defense may trigger multinational capital expenditure shifts and adjustment in global supply chain exposure, balancing both threat and opportunity for US-domiciled manufacturing.
  • Crypto ETFs set the stage for broader retail and institutional inflows, even as regulatory headlines may trigger spikes in volatility.

In summary: Expect modest but positive US growth, sticky inflation, incremental monetary easing, and continued policy/geopolitical uncertainty—favoring some risk assets, real assets, and secular winners in tech, semiconductors, and renewables, while staying alert for policy shocks and non-negligible macro/geopolitical risk events.
