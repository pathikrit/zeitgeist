Second/Third-Order Effects

War in Iran — Impact Pathways

  • Energy: High risk of further oil/gas infrastructure hits; attack on Kharg terminal and/or Persian Gulf refineries would spike Brent/WTI and spike global fuel input costs. Immediate oil price pullbacks should be seen as tactical, not durable.
  • Shipping/Trade: Extended conflict/Strait of Hormuz closure likely to disrupt expeditionary shipping, boost tanker rates, and add global inflation pressure. Oil consumers (Asia, EU) vulnerable to higher input costs.
  • Treasuries/FX: Risk premium likely to stay bid. Any US escalation/spike in oil could prompt late Q1/FY26 flight-to-quality trade (bonds, USD, gold, defensive equities).
  • Equities/Sectors: Weak for airlines, shipping/cargo, EM, and EU cyclicals. Energy producers, defense, cybersecurity, and gold miners favored on re-risking. Margins for discretionary/retail could get squeezed by input/fuel pass-through.
  • Policy: Congressional debate on war powers heightens near-term US government policy risk—possible spending surges, delayed budget resolution, and higher defense outlays. Senate/House votes on war could polarize; plausible that anti-war rhetoric returns to 2024–2025 levels.
  • Iran Succession: Uncertainty around Khamenei’s succession (Mojtaba Khamenei seen as favorite) could destabilize flows into regional EM, add a risk premium to Turkey, Qatar, UAE, and Saudi capital markets.

China (Two Sessions) – Global Supply Chains

  • Focus remains defensive: investment in R&D, domestic semis, and advanced industrials is prioritized, but unlikely to produce meaningful demand bump near term. Exporters face sluggish global backdrops (EU, LatAm); watch for infrastructure-focused stimulus in H2 if growth stalls.
  • Little relief for China-linked supply chains or EM commodity exporters while NPC remains risk-averse.
